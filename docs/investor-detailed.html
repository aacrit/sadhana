1. Executive Summary

Sādhanā (Sanskrit: साधना — disciplined practice toward mastery) is the world's first browser-based Hindustani classical music training application built on a comprehensive music physics engine. It transforms the student's device into an interactive instrument that listens, responds, and teaches — all at $0 operational cost.

The core insight: Existing music apps teach Western music theory or treat Indian music as a subset of Western frameworks. Sādhanā builds from first principles: 22 shrutis (microtonal intervals from the Natya Shastra), just intonation frequency ratios, raga grammar rules, and the oral-tradition pedagogy of Hindustani classical music. A musicologist opening the source code should find it as complete and rigorous as a textbook.

2. The Problem & Market Opportunity

The Gap

Hindustani classical music (HCM) has an estimated 300+ million practitioners across South Asia, with a global Indian diaspora of 32 million seeking cultural connection. Yet the digital tools available to them are fundamentally broken:

  • No digital tool teaches raga grammar — the rules of melodic movement (aroha/avaroha, vakra phrases, varjit swaras) that define a raga's identity. Existing apps treat ragas as scales.
  • No app uses just intonation — the mathematically correct tuning system where intervals are exact frequency ratios (3:2 for Pa, 5:4 for Ga). Every music app on the market uses equal temperament, which is a compromise designed for keyboard instruments and is fundamentally wrong for Indian music.
  • No platform provides context-aware pitch feedback — understanding that komal Re in Bhairav (where it carries andolan) is a different musical object from komal Re in Kafi (where it's a passing note).
  • Western music apps (Yousician, Simply Piano, Smule) serve 50M+ users but cannot teach Indian music correctly because their engines are built on 12-tone equal temperament.

3. Product Vision & Philosophy

THE ENGINE IS MUSIC. MUSIC IS THE ENGINE.
This is not a music learning app that uses audio. This is a music physics engine — the equivalent of NVIDIA PhysX, but for sound. The engine does not imitate music. It does not simulate music. It IS music.

Core Principles

  1. Audio Before Everything (The Presence Rule): Every concept is heard before it is named. Every time. The student meets a raga through its sound, not its theory. Labels arrive only after the ear has already formed an impression.
  2. Hindustani-First: Rooted in HCM tradition. Western equivalents are bridges for context, never the frame. Sa is not "C" — it is whatever the performer's voice naturally settles on.
  3. $0 End-to-End: No paid APIs. No paid services. No paid inference. Everything that requires compute runs in the browser (AudioWorklet, WebAssembly, Tone.js). Everything that requires storage uses Supabase free tier.
  4. Journeys Serve the Engine: The four journey interfaces adapt to the student. The engine never compromises its musical accuracy.
  5. The Sādhanā Principle: 5–15 minute daily sessions. One new musical capability per session. Mastery through consistency, not cramming.

5. The Music Engine — Deep Dive

5.1 The 22 Shrutis — Microtonal Foundation

The Natya Shastra (c. 200 BCE – 200 CE) codified 22 microtonal intervals within the octave. These are not arbitrary cultural choices — they emerge from the harmonic series. Every shruti is a ratio of small integers, and its musical character is a direct consequence of how its partials align with Sa's partials.

5.5 Tanpura Synthesis

The tanpura is not a sample — it is synthesised from first principles. A mathematical model of four vibrating strings, each producing a fundamental and a series of overtones shaped by the jivari (curved bridge):

  • 4 strings: Pa(low), Sa, Sa, Sa(lower octave). Alternative tuning: Ma replaces Pa for Ma-dominant ragas.
  • 12 partials per string: Amplitudes derived from jivari bridge coupling model.
  • Characteristic shimmer from: (a) rich overtone content, (b) 2-cent detuning between the two Sa strings, (c) natural beating between overlapping partials.
  • Pluck cycle: 4-second cycle (1.0s per string), configurable tempo.

Pitch Mapping — 7-Step Process

  1. Receive Hz from Pitchy with clarity confidence (0–1)
  2. Filter low-clarity detections (<0.85 threshold)
  3. Normalise Hz to Sa octave using student's stored Sa reference
  4. Compute cents deviation from each of the 12 swaras
  5. Select nearest swara with smallest absolute cents deviation
  6. Apply raga context: validate swara against current raga's aroha/avaroha
  7. Return PitchResult: swara, cents deviation, accuracy score, raga compliance

7. Tantri — The Instrument Layer

Tantri (तन्त्री — string of a veena) is the bridge between the music engine's mathematics and the human ear. It is not a component. It is the instrument itself.

Architecture

12 horizontal strings, one per chromatic swara, positioned by just-intonation frequency ratio on a logarithmic scale. Each string is simultaneously:

  • INPUT: Vibrates when the student's voice is near that swara's frequency. Colour encodes accuracy (saffron = perfect, green = good, amber = approaching). Amplitude encodes vocal intensity.
  • OUTPUT: Touch/click triggers harmonium synthesis. Kan snap on contact, Andolan sustain, Tanpura Release on release.

Spring Physics (Ragamala Motion Grammar)

Tanpura Release40015Natural decay after pluck (~800ms), page load stagger
Andolan1208Sustained gentle oscillation, hover states

Sympathetic Vibration

When Sa is sung, Pa vibrates sympathetically — just as a real tanpura string would. This is real physics: the 3:2 ratio creates natural resonance. The engine calculates sympathetic amplitudes for all consonant intervals.

8. User Lifecycle: Onboarding to Mastery

8.1 Onboarding (2 minutes)

  1. Sa Detection: The app asks the student to hum or sing "Sa" 3–5 times. The engine averages Pitchy detections and proposes: "Your Sa is G3 (196 Hz) — does that feel right?" Student confirms or adjusts. If skipped: defaults to C4 (261.63 Hz). Sa is stored in user profile and used as the root for all frequency calculations across the entire engine.
  2. Journey Selection: Four paths are visible immediately. The full vision is apparent from day one. The student sees Shishya through Guru — they know where they're going.
  3. First Riyaz: Within 90 seconds of opening the app, the tanpura is playing and the student is singing. Zero configuration required.

8.2 Daily Session Loop

  1. On open: The app has already prepared today's riyaz. Raga is chosen by time of day:
    • Dawn (6–9am) → Bhairav
    • Morning (9am–12pm) → Bhoopali
    • Afternoon (12–4pm) → Bhimpalasi
    • Evening (4–7pm) → Yaman
    • Night (7pm–6am) → Bageshri
  2. Tanpura begins. Student sings. ~10 minutes. Zero decisions required.
  3. On completion: Transitions to free practice. Daily goal ring updates on home page.
  4. If already done: Goes straight to free practice. No friction between ritual and freedom.

8.4 The Pakad Recognition Moment

When the engine detects that the student has sung a raga's characteristic phrase (pakad), a 2-layer cinematic moment unfolds:

  1. Layer 1 — Cinematic pause (~4s): Tanpura continues uninterrupted. Background deepens. Jali pattern reveals fully. Raga name appears large in Cormorant Garamond, center screen. Below it: the phrase in sargam notation (e.g., "Ni Re Ga Ma Ga Re Sa"). Fades slowly via GSAP timeline.
  2. Layer 2 — Settles: "You just sang the pakad of Yaman" remains at the bottom as a quiet record. Fades to 40% opacity. Stays for the rest of the session.

The tanpura never stops. Practice continues through it. This moment is unrepeatable in its first-time impact.

Raga Deep Dive: Darbari Kanada

One of the most complex ragas in the catalog, attributed to Tansen (court musician of Akbar). Demonstrates the engine's depth:

  • Vakra avaroha (non-linear descent): S' d n d P m P g m R S — the avaroha does not simply descend, it weaves
  • Andolan on Ga_komal IS the raga's identity — a slow, profound oscillation that defines Darbari
  • 5 pakad phrases encoded with swara-level precision
  • 4 gharana variations: Kirana (sustained andolan), Agra (gamak-heavy), Jaipur-Atrauli (geometric phrases), Gwalior (balanced)

14. Pedagogical Framework & References

The Oral Tradition, Digitised

Hindustani classical music has been transmitted through the guru-shishya parampara (teacher-student tradition) for centuries. Sādhanā does not replace the guru — it provides the daily structured practice that makes guru sessions more productive.

Pedagogical Principles

  1. The Presence Rule: Hear before label. Every new musical concept is experienced through sound before it receives a name or explanation. The student's ear forms an impression; only then does the theory arrive to confirm what they already feel.
  2. Progressive Disclosure: The interface reveals complexity only as the student earns it through musical acts. Strings appear on Tantri without announcement. The cents needle becomes available. New ragas surface. The student notices one day — that discovery is the ceremony.
  3. Spaced Consistency: Mastery gates require passing challenges across multiple sessions with cooldown periods (4–6 hours). This prevents cramming and enforces the Sādhanā principle: mastery comes from daily practice, not marathon sessions.
  4. Raga as Teacher: Each raga teaches a specific musical concept through its own nature. Bhoopali teaches pentatonic simplicity. Bhairav teaches komal swaras. Darbari teaches that ornament IS structure. The curriculum follows the ragas' natural teaching order.
  5. Error as Exploration: The system never says "wrong." It shows where you are relative to where the raga lives. Deviation is information, not failure.
